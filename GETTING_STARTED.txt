Haysey's Astrostacker -- Getting Started Guide
===============================================
For complete beginners. No experience required.


------------------------------------------------------------------------
WHAT IS IMAGE STACKING?
------------------------------------------------------------------------

Astrophotography images straight from the camera are dim and noisy.
A single exposure only captures a fraction of the signal available --
the rest is buried in random noise from the sensor and sky background.

Stacking solves this by combining many exposures of the same target.
Each frame captures slightly different random noise, but the real
signal (stars, nebulae, galaxies) stays in the same place. When you
average them together, the noise cancels out and the signal builds up.

Rule of thumb:
  10 frames  -- a noticeable improvement over a single shot
  20-30 frames -- a good result for most targets
  50+ frames -- excellent, especially for faint objects
  100+ frames -- outstanding (deep-sky imaging standard)


------------------------------------------------------------------------
BEFORE YOU BEGIN -- WHAT YOU NEED
------------------------------------------------------------------------

You need:

  1. A set of light frames
     These are your main exposures -- the images you took of your
     target (nebula, galaxy, star cluster, etc.).
     Format: FITS (.fits, .fit, .fts) or XISF (.xisf)

     Most astronomy capture software (SharpCap, NINA, Ekos, Sequence
     Generator Pro, FireCapture) saves in FITS format automatically.

  2. Optionally: dark frames and flat frames
     These calibration frames improve your result but are not required
     for your first attempt. See the CALIBRATION FRAMES section below.

You do NOT need:
  - Any programming knowledge
  - Python installed
  - Any other software

STEP 1 -- FIRST LAUNCH: THE SETUP WIZARD
------------------------------------------------------------------------

The first time you open Haysey's Astrostacker, a Setup Wizard appears.
It takes about 2 minutes and helps you get started quickly.

  Page 1 -- Welcome
    Read through the introduction and click Next.

  Page 2 -- Your Camera
    Choose whether your camera is a colour or monochrome camera.

    COLOUR camera: Canon, Nikon, Sony DSLRs and mirrorless cameras,
      and any astronomy camera with "colour" or "MC" in the name
      (e.g. ZWO ASI294MC, ASI1600MC). Your images look like normal
      photos in colour.

    MONOCHROME camera: A dedicated astronomy camera with a black-and-
      white sensor, typically used with separate colour filters.
      Model names often end in "MM" or "M" (e.g. ASI294MM).

    Not sure? If your images look like colour photographs straight
    from the camera, choose Colour.

  Page 3 -- Plate Solving (Optional)
    Plate solving identifies exactly where your image points in the sky
    and labels the objects it contains. This is completely optional.

    To set it up:
      - Get a free API key at https://nova.astrometry.net
        (click Sign Up, then find your key on My Profile)
      - Enter your telescope focal length (printed on the tube,
        e.g. f=335mm means enter 335)
      - Enter your camera pixel size (found in your camera's spec sheet
        or product page -- e.g. ZWO ASI294MC Pro = 4.63 µm)

    You can skip this page and set it up later in the Plate Solve tab.

  Page 4 -- You're Ready!
    A quick-start guide. Read through it, then click Let's Go!

You can re-run the wizard any time from Tools > Setup Wizard.


------------------------------------------------------------------------
STEP 2 -- LOAD YOUR LIGHT FRAMES
------------------------------------------------------------------------

Light frames are your main exposures. The app needs at least 2 of them
(though more is always better).

  1. In the Stacking tab on the left sidebar, find the Light Frames
     section at the top.

  2. Click the Add button (or the small down-arrow for more options).

  3. Navigate to the folder where your images are saved and select
     them all. You can select multiple files at once with
     Ctrl+Click (Windows/Linux) or Cmd+Click (Mac).

  4. Alternatively, drag and drop an entire folder directly onto the
     Light Frames list.

  5. The files appear in the list with a count at the top, e.g.
     "Light Frames (24)".

Tips:
  - Only include frames of the same target, same exposure, same camera
    settings. Do not mix different nights or targets in one stack.
  - FITS and XISF formats are supported. RAW files from Canon/Nikon
    are not supported -- use your capture software to save as FITS.


------------------------------------------------------------------------
STEP 3 -- ADD CALIBRATION FRAMES (OPTIONAL)
------------------------------------------------------------------------

Calibration frames improve your result by removing sensor noise and
optical imperfections. They are optional but recommended.

  DARK FRAMES
    Dark frames are exposures taken with the lens cap on (no light),
    at the same exposure time and camera temperature as your lights.
    They capture the thermal noise your sensor adds to every image.

    How to take them: Put the lens cap on. Take 10-20 exposures at
    the same exposure time and ISO/gain as your light frames.

    To load in the app:
      - In the Dark Frames section, click Add and select your darks.
      - OR if you already have a pre-built master dark file, click
        Master Dark... to load just that single file.

  FLAT FRAMES
    Flat frames are taken of an evenly lit surface (dawn sky, white
    panel, or dedicated flat panel). They correct for vignetting
    (dark corners), dust spots, and uneven illumination.

    How to take them: Point your telescope at an evenly lit surface
    and take 20-30 short exposures (aim for ~40-50% brightness on
    your histogram).

    To load in the app:
      - In the Flat Frames section, click Add and select your flats.

You do not need calibration frames for your very first test stack.
Add them once your basic workflow is working.


------------------------------------------------------------------------
STEP 4 -- CHECK YOUR SETTINGS
------------------------------------------------------------------------

The Settings panel is in the lower right of the main window.

  Camera Type (top of settings)
    Set by the wizard, but double-check this matches your camera.
    Choose Colour (Bayer) for DSLRs and colour astronomy cameras.
    Choose Mono for dedicated monochrome astronomy cameras.

  Stacking Method
    For your first stack, leave this set to Median. It is the safest
    and most reliable option for any number of frames.

    Once you have more experience:
      Sigma Clip is better for 15+ frames -- it removes satellite
      trails, planes, and hot pixels automatically.

  Output Path
    This is where your stacked image will be saved.
    The default is "stacked.fits" in the same folder you launched from.
    Click Browse to choose a different location.

  Processing Options
    These are all optional. For your first stack, leave them all off.

    When you are ready to explore them:
      Auto-reject blurry/trailed frames: Recommended. Automatically
        removes poor-quality frames before stacking.

      Remove light pollution gradient: Turn this on if your result
        has uneven brightness or a bright corner (common in cities).

      Auto-crop stacking edges: Cleans up the dark borders created
        by frame alignment. Recommended.

      Denoise: Smooths noisy backgrounds. Start with Light.

      Sharpen: Tightens star profiles. Start with Light.


------------------------------------------------------------------------
STEP 5 -- START STACKING
------------------------------------------------------------------------

  1. Click the Start Processing button (the large orange outlined button
     at the bottom of the Settings panel).

  2. The progress log in the centre of the screen shows what the app is
     doing -- calibrating frames, aligning them, and stacking.

  3. Wait for it to finish. How long it takes depends on:
       - The number of frames (more = longer)
       - Your computer speed
       - Whether post-processing options are enabled
     Typical times: 2-10 minutes for 20-50 frames on a modern computer.

  4. A chime sounds when processing is complete.

  5. Your stacked image appears in the Preview panel (top right).


------------------------------------------------------------------------
STEP 6 -- VIEW AND EXPORT YOUR RESULT
------------------------------------------------------------------------

  The Preview Panel (top right)
    Your stacked image is shown here with an automatic brightness
    stretch applied so you can see the detail.

    Use the zoom buttons to zoom in and inspect your image.

    Use the stretch toggle (Auto/Linear) to switch between stretched
    and linear (unstretched) views.

  The Histogram Panel
    Shows the pixel brightness distribution of your image. Useful for
    checking exposure and checking that the stack worked correctly.

  Exporting your image

    To save a shareable image (e.g. to post online):
      File > Export as TIFF  -- best quality, large file
      File > Export as PNG   -- good quality, smaller file

      Both options apply an automatic brightness stretch so the image
      looks like a normal photo. This is the file you share.

    Your raw stacked FITS file is also saved automatically to the
    Output Path you set in the Settings panel. This is the science-
    quality data file for use in PixInsight, Siril, or other tools.


------------------------------------------------------------------------
STEP 7 -- PLATE SOLVING (OPTIONAL)
------------------------------------------------------------------------

Plate solving tells you exactly where in the sky your image points.
The app does this using the free Astrometry.net service online.

Why do it?
  - Labels the objects in your image (NGC, Messier, stars, etc.)
  - Embeds WCS coordinates into your FITS file -- required by
    PixInsight's SPCC (colour calibration) and other tools
  - Required for mosaic stitching

How to set it up (one time):
  1. Go to https://nova.astrometry.net and sign up for a free account
  2. Go to My Profile (top right) and copy your API Key
  3. Click the Plate Solve tab in the left sidebar
  4. Paste your API key into the API Key field
  5. Enter your telescope focal length (mm) and camera pixel size (µm)
     in the FOV Calculator section, then click Calculate & Apply

How to find your focal length and pixel size:
  Focal length: printed on the telescope tube (e.g. "f=335mm")
                or in the manual. The number after "f=" in millimetres.
  Pixel size:   search "[your camera model] specifications" online and
                look for Pixel Size or Pixel Pitch (in micrometres, µm).
                Examples: ZWO ASI294MC Pro = 4.63 µm, Canon 80D = 3.71 µm

How to plate solve:
  1. Click the Plate Solve tab
  2. Click Browse and select your stacked FITS file (or it may already
     be loaded if you just finished stacking)
  3. Click Solve and wait -- this can take 2-10 minutes online
  4. Results appear in the log and an annotated image opens

The app remembers your API key, focal length, and pixel size. You only
need to enter these once.


------------------------------------------------------------------------
TIPS FOR BETTER RESULTS
------------------------------------------------------------------------

More frames = better noise reduction. 20-30 is a good starting point.
Take darks. Even 10 dark frames make a noticeable difference.
Use a consistent setup. Same exposure, gain, temperature each session.
Auto-reject works best with 6+ frames. With fewer frames, turn it off.
Gradient removal helps a lot from suburban locations.
Start with Median stacking. Try Sigma Clip once you have 15+ frames.
Enable Auto-crop to get a clean rectangular result.
Try Denoise (Light) to smooth noisy backgrounds without losing detail.
Try Sharpen (Light) on a good stack to tighten star profiles.
Save your session (File > Save Session) before closing, so you can
  reload all your file lists and settings next time.

COMMON QUESTIONS
------------------------------------------------------------------------

Q: The stacked image looks blurry or washed out.
A: This is normal for a raw stack without stretching. The Preview panel
   applies an auto-stretch to show the detail. For a brighter, sharper-
   looking image, export using File > Export as TIFF.

Q: My frames failed to align.
A: Alignment needs at least 5-10 stars in each frame. Very short
   exposures or heavily overexposed images may not have enough.
   Try increasing your exposure time, or check your frames aren't
   totally saturated.

Q: Plate solving failed or timed out.
A: Make sure you entered your telescope focal length and camera pixel
   size in the FOV Calculator (Plate Solve tab > Scale Hints section)
   and clicked Calculate & Apply. Without these, plate solving can
   take 10+ minutes or fail entirely.

Q: I get a "No stars detected" error.
A: Your images may be too short (underexposed) or the focus may be
   very poor. The app needs to detect at least a few stars in each
   frame for alignment to work.

Q: The result has dark borders around the edges.
A: This is normal -- frame alignment shifts frames slightly, leaving
   blank edges. Tick "Auto-crop stacking edges" in the Settings panel
   to remove them automatically.

Q: Where is my stacked file saved?
A: To the path shown in Output Path in the Settings panel. The default
   is "stacked.fits" in the folder where you launched the app.
